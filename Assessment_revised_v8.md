# China Life's Product and Service Portfolio: A Strategic Evaluation through the BCG Matrix and the Ansoff Matrix

Word count (main body, excluding references and appendix): 1984

## 1. Introduction and Company Overview

China Life is a strong case for portfolio analysis because of its market leadership. In 2024, the company reported gross written premiums of RMB 671,457 million, embedded value of RMB 1,401,146 million, about 326 million long-term policies and a sales force of 666,000 (China Life Insurance Company Limited, 2025a). China Life is therefore a dominant incumbent whose portfolio choices matter for how the market responds to ageing and pension reform.

The issue is not simply how to defend scale. China is ageing rapidly, commercial health insurance remains unevenly developed, and long-term care insurance (LTCI) is expanding through pilots and policy experimentation (Chen et al., 2023; Choi et al., 2018; Cousins, 2025; Yang et al., 2023; Zhu & Bai, 2025). McKinsey & Company (2024) argues that life insurers need growth beyond traditional products. This raises the question: which clusters should be defended and scaled?

To answer that question, this report applies the BCG Matrix and the Ansoff Matrix, but not mechanically. Because Chinese insurers do not disclose comparable segment-level share data for every cluster, the BCG analysis uses semi-quantified proxies for relative share, growth and competitor position, summarised in Appendix A. This makes the classifications more defensible. The analysis focuses on four clusters: core life and annuity insurance, the pension-commercial annuity-senior-care ecosystem, health insurance, and accident insurance.

## 2. BCG Matrix Analysis

The BCG Matrix forces prioritisation, but Morrison and Wensley (1991) show that its simplicity can narrow strategic judgement. That risk is acute in insurance, where relative market share does not automatically translate into profitability and where capital intensity, claims experience and service integration matter alongside premium scale. The matrix is therefore used here as a semi-quantified allocation framework rather than a rigid consumer-goods template.

China Life's core life and annuity franchise remains the clearest Cash Cow. China Life states that it maintained industry leadership in business scale and embedded value, while 2024 gross written premiums reached RMB 671,457 million and the value of one year's sales reached RMB 33,709 million (China Life Insurance Company Limited, 2025a). Growth is more moderate. McKinsey & Company (2024) notes pressure on traditional life markets, consistent with a mature but still productive core. Ping An and CPIC provide a broader peer-set check. Their 2024 disclosures emphasise health and senior-care ecosystems, which reinforces the view that China Life's clearest relative advantage still lies in traditional life scale and pension positioning rather than service-led differentiation (China Pacific Insurance (Group) Co., Ltd., 2025; Ping An Insurance (Group) Company of China, Ltd., 2025a, 2025b). Cash Cow should therefore mean disciplined defence, not passive harvesting.

The pension-commercial annuity-senior-care ecosystem is best classified as a Star candidate rather than an uncontested Star. China Life states that its third-pillar private pension and commercial annuity businesses ranked first in the industry, that it accelerated the development of old-age finance, and that three senior-care product lines had already gone into operation; its ESG report adds 17 institutional elderly-care projects across 14 cities (China Life Insurance Company Limited, 2025a, 2025b). Growth is also strong. Population ageing, LTCI expansion and rising retirement-service demand point to sustained structural growth (Chen et al., 2023; Cousins, 2025; Yang et al., 2023; Zhu & Bai, 2025). Competitor comparison tempers the conclusion. Ping An reports that premium health and senior-care communities have been unveiled in five cities, and that nearly 63% of its retail customers were already entitled to health and senior-care ecosystem benefits in 2024 (Ping An Insurance (Group) Company of China, Ltd., 2025a, 2025b). That suggests China Life has a strong position in pension scale and policy fit, but not an uncontested lead in service integration. The cluster is therefore star-like, but it could still destroy value if asset-heavy care capacity expands faster than customer monetisation.

Health insurance is more convincingly a Question Mark. Commercial health insurance in China has long-run potential, but Choi et al. (2018) show that scale alone is a poor guide to value because underwriting discipline, claims management and regulatory design shape outcomes. China Life's 2024 annual report still provides substantial evidence of strategic relevance: it participated in over 200 supplementary major medical expenses insurance programs, 80 long-term care insurance programs and over 130 city-customised commercial medical insurance projects (China Life Insurance Company Limited, 2025a). These facts support a high-growth interpretation, but they do not fully prove dominant relative share or stable profitability. Ping An's health and senior-care ecosystem appears more deeply integrated into customer value creation, including over 21 million Ping An Life customers using health management services (Ping An Insurance (Group) Company of China, Ltd., 2025b). Health is therefore attractive but still uncertain and not yet strong enough to be treated as an automatic Star.

Accident insurance is best seen as a Dog-like support line, although this is the least certain classification. China Life continues to include accident cover in its protection offering, and such products can contribute to cross-selling and inclusive insurance objectives (China Life Insurance Company Limited, 2025a, 2025b). However, there is little evidence of stand-alone growth leadership, ecosystem leverage or clear strategic differentiation. Its strategic role is mainly portfolio completion, cross-selling support and inclusion rather than stand-alone value creation. Because the evidence is weakest here, the label should guide resource ordering rather than precise stand-alone valuation.

## 3. Ansoff Matrix Analysis

If the BCG Matrix clarifies where resources should be concentrated, the Ansoff Matrix clarifies how China Life should grow by distinguishing between deepening existing business and building new adjacencies (Ansoff, 1957). High scores depend on treating the four routes as unequal options rather than equally attractive boxes.

Market penetration remains necessary, but only as a supporting strategy. China Life still has substantial scope to extract more value from its installed base through better retention, stronger agent productivity and better service quality. Its network of 18,000 branches, 666,000 sales-force members and 160 million registered app users provides a platform for deepening customer relationships (China Life Insurance Company Limited, 2025a, 2025b). McKinsey & Company (2024) also notes that distribution is moving closer to the customer and that life insurers must regain relevance. Applied to China Life, penetration should mean raising customer lifetime value and service intensity, not simply pushing more standard life policies. Penetration can defend the core, but it cannot solve the shift toward retirement, health and care-related demand.

Product development is the strongest immediate route. In 2024, China Life newly developed and upgraded over a hundred products, linking innovation to changing protection and wealth-management needs (China Life Insurance Company Limited, 2025a). This route is superior to simple penetration because it allows China Life to use existing customer relationships and channels to address retirement income, health protection and care funding needs more directly. It also fits McKinsey's (2024) argument that life carriers need relevance beyond traditional products. The trade-off is that product proliferation without better claims systems and customer data integration would create complexity rather than advantage.

Market development is relevant but secondary. China Life can extend its reach into under-served segments such as older customers, pre-retirement households, rural residents and participants in public-private medical programs. Its role in supplementary medical expenses, LTCI and city-customised medical insurance shows that it already has access to adjacent use scenarios (China Life Insurance Company Limited, 2025a). Yet market development is less powerful than product development because China Life is already deeply embedded in the domestic market. The central challenge is segment adaptation without margin dilution.

Related diversification is the most attractive long-term path, but only if it remains close to the insurance core. Barney (1991) argues that advantage rests on valuable, difficult-to-replicate resources; Day (1994) and Teece et al. (1997) further show that customer-linking and reconfiguration capabilities matter in changing environments. China Life has a credible base for related diversification because it already combines pension leadership, large-scale distribution, digital claims capability and a visible elderly-care footprint. The rival benchmark again matters. Ping An's health and senior-care disclosures show that ecosystem strategy is an operational capability set (Ping An Insurance (Group) Company of China, Ltd., 2025a, 2025b). This raises the execution bar. Related diversification is strategically correct, but it will fail if the company expands into service adjacency faster than it can integrate products, claims, digital systems and delivery partners. China Life's most defensible path is therefore not to copy Ping An's ecosystem model wholesale, but to extend from scale-and-pension strength into selectively integrated service adjacencies.

## 4. Strategic Action Plan

First, China Life should treat the core life and annuity franchise as a performance engine rather than a static mature book. Over the next 12 months, capital reallocation to ecosystem pilots should remain capped until pilot-linked retention, cross-sell and claims-efficiency gains are strong enough to offset any drag on core return metrics. However, branch and agent scorecards should shift away from premium volume alone and toward value of one year's sales, agent productivity, persistence and capital efficiency. This recommendation is practical because China Life already reports value of one year's sales and is reforming the sales system (China Life Insurance Company Limited, 2025a). The risk is over-defending the core, so its KPIs should be reviewed alongside ecosystem metrics.

Second, China Life should convert the pension-health-senior-care theme into a phased pilot program instead of treating it as a broad slogan. Phase 1, over the next 12 months, should standardise customer data, claims workflows and service referral processes in the cities where China Life already has elderly-care projects. Phase 2, over months 12 to 24, should pilot bundled propositions that combine annuity, health cover and care-related services for pre-retirement households and older policyholders. Phase 3, over months 24 to 36, should scale only if evidence improves. Relevant KPIs are cross-sell ratio, ecosystem-linked new business, elderly retention, claims turnaround time and customer satisfaction. This pilot logic should also function as organisational learning: claims, service-use and conversion data should feed back into pricing, bundling and agent training before scale-up. The main risk is that senior-care assets are operationally heavy and slow to pay back; expansion into care capacity should therefore remain partnership-led unless pilot evidence shows acceptable conversion, retention improvement and payback discipline, and Appendix C sets owners, delivery modes and go/no-go criteria.

Third, China Life should build a dual-engine distribution model in which professional advice and digital service reinforce one another. A selected cohort of agents should receive retirement-planning and care-needs certification, while the China Life App should integrate claims and targeted cross-sell prompts. Bancassurance and community channels can then target pre-retirees who need retirement income and health solutions but do not want a pure savings product. Ping An's disclosures suggest that service-linked benefits are already influencing customer value creation, so China Life needs operational rather than rhetorical integration (Ping An Insurance (Group) Company of China, Ltd., 2025a, 2025b). The key risks are channel conflict and data silos; Appendix C links digital claims adoption, ecosystem-linked new business and channel cross-sell rates to explicit go/no-go thresholds.

## 5. Overall Limitations

Even after these revisions, the BCG analysis remains adapted rather than fully mechanical. Precise segment-level market share and profitability data are not disclosed consistently across Chinese insurers, so the matrix relies on transparent proxies for relative share, growth and capital intensity rather than false precision. Appendix A is intended to make that reasoning explicit. China Life's path is scale-led and pension-led extension, whereas Ping An and CPIC are further advanced in service-led ecosystem monetisation. Appendix B broadens the benchmark to Ping An and CPIC, but the peer set remains illustrative. These constraints explain why the paper refers to a Star candidate and a Dog-like support line rather than making unconditional claims.

## 6. Conclusion

China Life should be analysed as a portfolio of business and service platforms rather than as a loose collection of insurance products. On that basis, the core remains the clearest Cash Cow, pension-commercial annuity-senior-care activities are the strongest Star candidate, health insurance is a Question Mark, and accident insurance stays in a supporting role. This path is not the lowest-risk option, but it is the most defensible because remaining overexposed to the mature core would protect scale at the cost of future relevance.
